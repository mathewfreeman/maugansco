Her Legacy

Hadley's Story

Hadley Huffman was born in Ann Arbor, Michigan, and grew up attending Pioneer High School before continuing her education at the University of Arizona and later Wayne State University.

From a young age, Hadley demonstrated a deep commitment to equity, using her voice and platform to advocate for marginalized communities. During her time at Wayne State, she participated in a medical volunteer program in Portugal that fundamentally shaped her perspective on global healthcare disparities.

This experience, coupled with her passion for equity, led her to establish Students Against Medical Racism, an initiative set in the belief that accessible, equitable, and affordable healthcare is a fundamental human right, not a privilege.

Hadley aspired to become a doctor with the goal of joining Doctors Without Borders. Although she passed away in 2021, her vision lives on. This foundation was created by her family, friends, and the community she inspired to continue her mission of addressing systemic barriers and advocating for meaningful change.

Core Directives

Advancing equality in healthcare by confronting discrimination.

Advocacy

We advocate for a world where healthcare is a right, not a privilege. By lending our voice to support policies, initiatives, and organizations that fight systemic barriers to healthcare, we promote equitable access for underserved communities.

Community Engagement

We believe lasting change begins in the community. By listening, partnering, and learning from those most affected, we help amplify local voices and support solutions that truly meet the needs of our mission.

Everyone deserves quality healthcare. We work to ensure access is fair, regardless of race, class, or background.

II.

Empowerment

We strengthen organizations driving change, giving them the tools and support to make a real, lasting impact.

III.

Education

We raise awareness and provide resources to inform the public about systemic inequities within the healthcare field.

IV.

Stewardship

We responsibly manage our resources to maximize positive change, ensuring every donation drives meaningful progress.

Research for Equity in Care

We curate and advance research on racial bias in medicine and unequal access in underprivileged communities. We aim to amplify the work of organizations and researchers to educate the public on the lasting impacts of discrimination.

Racial and ethnic discrimination in health care was reported to be highest in the United States.

Note: Discrimination in the health system was defined as physicians who said the health care system "very often" or "often" treats people unfairly based on race or ethnicity or responded "yes" when asked if their patients have ever told them that because of their racial or ethnic background they felt they were treated unfairly or their health concerns were not taken as seriously by a health care professional.

Racial Disparities in Outcomes

Globally, disparities are striking. In the US, Black women are three times more likely to die from pregnancy-related complications than White women, and minority infants face mortality rates more than twice as high.

What Can Be Done

Implicit bias training, curricula revisions, standardized clinical processes, and diverse healthcare leadership.

Sorice, V. et al. The Perpetual Cycle of Racial Bias... J. Racial and Ethnic Health Disparities (2025). doi: 10.1007/s40615-025-02417-6

Gender Bias in Diagnosis

Women are 50% more likely to be misdiagnosed with a heart attack. Misconceptions of cardiovascular disease as a 'men's disease' contribute to under-diagnosis and treatment worldwide.

What Can Be Done

Develop interventions with a comprehensive, long-term gender perspective to tackle clinical bias across the continuum.

Al Hamid A. et al. Gender Bias in Diagnosis... Cureus. 2024. doi: 10.7759/cureus.54264.
Alcalde-Rubio, L. et al. Gender disparities in clinical practice... Int J Equity Health (2020).

Frontline Observations

Discrimination based on race, ethnicity, or language negatively impacts care quality. Workers on the front line of care delivery provide critical insights into the nature of this discrimination to help inform solutions.

Read the full article here Revealing Disparities: Health Care Workers' Observations of Discrimination Against Patients. Data: 2022 Commonwealth Fund International Health Policy Survey.

Medical Deserts

Access to maternity care and pharmacies remains inequitable, severely impacting over two million women in rural areas and disproportionately affecting racial and ethnic minority neighborhoods.

Fontenot, J., Brigance, C., et al. Navigating geographical disparities: access to obstetric hospitals in maternity care deserts... BMC Pregnancy Childbirth 24, 350 (2024).
Brantley, A., Mosha, D., et al. Pharmacy deserts and health inequities in St. Louis City... BMC Public Health 25, 4309 (2025).

The Perpetual Cycle of Bias

Racial bias in healthcare education is deeply ingrained, creating systemic barriers that significantly affect the quality and accessibility of care provided to marginalized groups.

Read the full article here Sorice, V. et al. The Perpetual Cycle of Racial Bias in Healthcare and Healthcare Education: A Systematic Review. (2025).

Implicit Bias

Associations outside conscious awareness lead to negative evaluations. Studies reveal this can directly affect treatment, such as variations in pain management and prescription dosing based solely on a patient's race.

FitzGerald, C., Hurst, S. Implicit bias in healthcare professionals: a systematic review. BMC Med Ethics 18, 19 (2017). https://doi.org/10.1186/s12910-017-0179-8
